Finding #2

The Salary Gap Is a Grade Story, Not a Sector Story

The federal government pays everyone on the General Schedule (GS) — grade determines salary, not sector. The apparent Agriculture vs Sustainability gap disappears once GS grade and location are controlled.

What is GS? The General Schedule is the federal pay scale. GS-5 is the most common entry-level grade — GS-7 and GS-9 are the next steps up. Pay is nearly deterministic by grade, locality, and year. Our regression R² = 0.947, which is expected given this structure.

Finding #3

The Barrier Is Structural, Not Individual

Rising technical demands mean the right credentials now determine whether an application reaches a human reviewer. Over half of postings show signs of ATS usage.

What is ATS? Applicant Tracking Systems are automated software that screen resumes before any human reads them. They filter by keywords, credentials, and tool names. The wrong keywords, a missing certification, or an absent tool name eliminates a qualified candidate invisibly — before a recruiter ever sees their name.

Summary

Key Takeaways

01

Active Learning Is the Only Skill Trending Upward

Every other skill category is declining or flat on the O*NET 5-point scale (2020→2024). Employers want candidates who can adapt — not candidates who already know everything. Active learning is rising because the field is changing too fast to require mastery of any fixed toolset.

02

Digital Tool Proficiency Is Now the Gatekeeper

GIS, data analytics, and remote sensing platforms entered 13 of 21 occupation categories since 2020 — for roles that didn't require them when current seniors enrolled. Students face ATS screening barriers based on credentials that postdate their curriculum choices. This is a structural misalignment, not an individual failure.

03

The Salary Story Is About Grade, Not Sector

The raw Agriculture vs Sustainability wage gap collapses to +0.4% (NS) once GS grade and location are controlled. Grade sorting explains the raw difference. Credentials that unlock GS-7 and GS-9 entry grades matter far more than sector choice — GIS and data analytics are the primary route to entering above GS-5.

Implications

Policy Implications

For Students
  • Build GIS and data analytics skills before graduation — now baseline screening criteria in 13 occupation categories
  • Pursue federal Pathways programs or private sector internships early — federal early-career rate is only 3.3%
  • Target GS-7 and GS-9 entry grades through certifications — +6.8% and +13.2% salary premiums
  • Optimize resumes for ATS keyword screening
For Universities
  • Embed GIS and data literacy across all agricultural and environmental programs — not just data tracks
  • Integrate ATS navigation into career services
  • Create structured pathways to internship access before students enter the job market
  • Align curriculum to the 86 new technology requirements now expected in federal and private roles
For Employers
  • Audit ATS settings to avoid filtering qualified early-career candidates whose credentials postdate enrollment
  • Recognize that GS grade and location — not sector — drive federal compensation differences
  • Invest in GIS and data literacy training for entry-level hires in roles that recently added these requirements

Systems of Equations
Equation 1 — Skill Composition Model (SUREG)
Skillⱼ = β₀ + β₁(After_ChatGPT) + β₂(Sector) + β₃(Location) + β₄(Job_Type) + εᵢ
where j ∈ {Technical, Digital, Soft} · company-clustered SE · HC1 robust standard errors
Equation 2 — Hedonic Salary Model (Two-Way Fixed Effects)
log(Salary) = β₀ + β₁(After_ChatGPT) + β₂(Sector) + β₃(Sector×After) + β₄(Grade) + αᵢ + γᵗ + δˡ + ε
αᵢ = company FE · γᵗ = year FE · δˡ = location FE · N=21,098 grade-known rows · R²=0.947

R² ≈ 0.95 is expected for federal GS data — grade, locality, and year nearly determine pay mechanically. This is not overfitting.
